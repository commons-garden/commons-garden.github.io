### ðŸ†• What's NEW This Time

**1. FOSS Volunteer Ecosystem Details** (Understanding Knowledge as a Commons, p.300)

- **3 types of motivations:**
  - **Technological:** Need software unavailable/too expensive, can't build alone
  - **Sociopolitical:** "Software should be free" philosophy (FSF), anti-monopoly stance
  - **Economic:** Building human capital (learning by doing), signaling expertise for jobs
- **~33% of FOSS developers are PAID by employers** to participate (Ghosh 2002)
- **"Passion" is critical driver** - projects must be interesting to attract/retain volunteers
- **Failure factors:** "inability to gain critical mass of eager volunteer programmers" + "lack of financing to pay for participants"
- **Credit is KEY norm:** Giving attribution to code authors enables reputation building

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** Mix of volunteer gardeners (passion-driven) + potential paid facilitators (sustainability-driven). Credit/attribution = essential for reputation economy.

---

**2. "Monitor the Monitor" Problem** (Understanding Institutional Diversity, p.251)

- **4 payment methods for forest guards:**
  1. Each household pays in kind (grains, services)
  2. Each household pays in cash
  3. Forest user organization pays from general funds
  4. Government distributes funds
- **"Farmers pay guards directly enables farmers to 'monitor' the monitor more effectively"**
- **Oath-taking rituals:** Guards taken to temple to "break a coconut" and vow impartiality before collecting Rs10/acre/month
- **Proportionality principle:** Benefits received must roughly match costs paid

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** If we pay facilitators, who monitors THEM? Direct payment by gardeners creates accountability loop. Ritual onboarding (oath equivalents) build commitment.

---

**3. Input Bias vs Outcome Focus** (The Samaritan's Dilemma, p.71)

- **Career concerns model (Holmstrom 1982):** Agents motivated by "demonstrating abilities" not immediate monetary rewards
- **Aid agencies:** Staff are salaried, not performance-linked â†’ strategic selection of tasks
- **Input bias:** Focus on easily monitored activities (reports, meetings, deliverables)
- **Outcome neglect:** Sustainability outcomes are "far more difficult to monitor" â†’ deprioritized

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** If we pay facilitators salary (vs outcome-based), they'll optimize for visible work (meeting notes, process docs) over hard-to-measure outcomes (trust building, conflict resolution quality, long-term community health).

---

**4. Consultant-Donor "In Bed" Problem** (The Samaritan's Dilemma, p.204)

- **Sida/Orissa forestry case:** "Consultants were in bed with Sida" - shared office, called "Sida Forestry Coordination Project"
- **Zero beneficiary ownership:** "Local communities had NO input into design, even though project seeks to invoke their participation"
- **Forest Department resistance:** De facto control already passed to communities â†’ reforms would reduce their power further
- **NGO role:** Vocal critics highlighting indigenous institutions, lobbying for community ownership

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** Risk if external funders (grants, sponsors) hire facilitators who are loyal to FUNDER not GARDENERS. Need beneficiary ownership from design phase, not just execution.

---

**5. NGO Conflicts: "Deep Green" vs Community-Oriented** (The Commons in the New Millennium, p.332)

- **Thailand Community Forestry Bill:** Two opposing NGO camps
  - **Community-oriented NGOs:** Northern Development Foundation, promoting People's Draft, advocating ethnic minorities in protected areas
  - **"Deep green" NGOs:** Environmental groups wanting to prohibit community forests in protected areas
- **Trigger events:** Formation of watershed groups driven by (a) water shortage, (b) protecting forests against private investors/state claims
- **Broader movement:** Civil rights, minority rights, anti-state bureaucracy, decentralization, anti-commercialization

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** If we engage external advocacy orgs, expect conflicts between "purity" groups (tech perfection, strict decentralization) vs "pragmatist" groups (community needs first). Water scarcity = our equivalent crisis that forces organization.

---

**6. Formal-Informal Substitution** (Linking the Formal and Informal Economy, p.231)

- **Voluntary contributions to local public goods:** Time, money, in-kind services to community associations
- **Government intervention paradox:** External funding can SUBSTITUTE (crowd out) or COMPLEMENT voluntary contributions
- **Challenge:** "If formal sector merely substitutes for informal, net benefits may be much smaller than when activities are complementary"
- **Optimal design:** Harness 'bonding' social capital while gaining access to 'bridging' capital

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** If we get grant funding, does it kill volunteer energy ("why should I contribute if they have money?") or amplify it (professional facilitators enable more volunteer participation)? Need complementary design.

---

**7. Microfinance Meetings as Governance Venue** (The Commons in the New Millennium, p.304)

- **Microcredit groups (Grameen Bank model):** Regular meetings create communication channels
- **Repurposing:** Meetings designed for loan accountability ALSO used to "monitor and amend day-to-day operational rules governing CPR use"
- **Women as primary CPR users:** If women manage forests/water, microfinance provides conduit for changing operational rules among themselves
- **"Bridging capital":** MFOs provide external assistance to break perverse logic when groups are blocked by powerful individuals

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** Our "show and tell" meetings (designed for demo/learning) could ALSO become governance venue. Women/marginalized gardeners might need separate spaces to develop rules before engaging broader community.

---

**8. User Attributes for Success** (People and Forests, p.258)

**From Ostrom's framework - 6 critical user attributes:**

- **A1. Salience:** Users depend on resource for major livelihood portion
- **A2. Common understanding:** Shared mental model of resource + how actions affect each other
- **A3. Low discount rate:** Value future benefits sufficiently
- **A4. Trust & reciprocity:** Users trust each other to keep promises
- **A5. Autonomy:** Users can determine rules without external authorities countermanding
- **A6. Prior organizational experience:** Learned skills through other local associations

ðŸ’¡ **Parallel to commons.garden:** Our intake questions map to these! "Why join?" (A1 salience), "What can you offer/need?" (A2 understanding), "Long term interest?" (A3 discount rate), "Done this before?" (A6 experience). Missing: explicit trust/autonomy questions.

**âš ï¸ FigJam's Innovations:**

- **"We pay people in meaning"** - Novel framing! Literature discusses "intrinsic motivation" but rarely as explicit compensation model
- **"We pay people with accreditation"** - Recognition/reputation as currency - seen in open-source commons but undertheorized in academic literature
- **"Help people pivot to work they care about"** - Not found in commons literature; more aligned with social entrepreneurship/career coaching

**ðŸ’¡ Literature Warns:**

- **Purely non-monetary compensation is risky** - Research on volunteer commons shows high failure rates due to:
  - Burnout (no boundaries on unpaid labor)
  - "Founder's trap" (few people do most work)
  - Class bias (only those with financial security can participate)
